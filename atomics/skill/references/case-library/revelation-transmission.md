# Case Library - Revelation and Transmission (RT-1 through RT-4)

Load this file only after the case has been identified as a revelation / transmission case, or as a
final-prophethood case whose live pressure is actually testimony, text, canon, preservation,
historical criticism, or original-text skepticism.
Begin with V10 (`references/techniques/V10-transmission-content-vetting.md`) before using any RT
entry. The family exists to keep testimony, textual integrity, canon authority, and believer
destabilization from being collapsed into one objection.

This family does not authorize debate-autonomous chaining after V10 or an RT sub-route lands.
Refresh the case-state after each cleared burden. If another live burden now governs, route
there explicitly. If not, release only the smallest restorative next move rather than treating
RT progression as permission to spill every downstream answer in one pass.

## RT-1 — Manuscript Reconstruction vs. Authenticated Transmission

*The specific question this case answers:* Does a manuscript artifact, fragment, or textual find overturn authenticated transmission?
*Evidence-type this operates on:* Physical manuscript evidence, fragment discoveries, textual variants, citation claims.

**The pressure:** "We found a manuscript / fragment / image / quotation that changes everything,"
or "historical criticism shows the original text is unrecoverable."

**Primary distinction:** Manuscript reconstruction is not the same thing as authenticated
transmission. A text-artifact may witness to a textual history without functioning as a
self-authenticating authority.

*Sub-route 1 — Provenance:* What exactly is this object? Where did it come from, how was it dated, by what method, and through whose hands did it reach the person presenting it? An artifact's dramatic appearance does not settle its provenance. Apply V10 step 1: identify the artifact's chain of custody, dating methodology, and institutional provenance before evaluating its claim.

*Sub-route 2 — Contents:* What does the artifact actually show? Many presented fragments show orthographic variation, not doctrinal difference. Distinguish: does the artifact contradict the transmitted text, supplement it, or show an expected scribal variant? The gap between "this fragment is different" and "this fragment disproves transmission" is the most common confusion. Apply V10 step 2: separate what the artifact textually contains from what is being imposed on it.

*Sub-route 3 — Authority:* Even if the artifact is authentic and its contents genuine, does it become normatively decisive? Authenticated transmission through mutawātir chains has its own epistemic weight. A single artifact cannot overturn a transmission whose epistemic force comes from mass simultaneous multi-path transmission — a different category from single-strand manuscript custody. Apply V10 step 3: is the artifact historically interesting or normatively decisive?

*Cross-tradition contamination guard:* NT-derived text-critical assumptions — reconstructive skepticism about manuscript variants, late-text dating heuristics, single-strand chain-of-custody reasoning — are calibrated to the specific conditions of the NT manuscript tradition: a gap of decades to centuries between original and earliest manuscript, anonymous transmission, and the absence of systematic preservation. These conditions do not apply to Qurʾānic transmission, which has a living mutawātir recitational community, an isnād system, and documented early preservation. Do not import NT skeptical heuristics into Qurʾānic cases.

**Restorative point:** A reconstruction problem and an authentication problem are not identical. Do not let a dramatic artifact bypass the question of transmission discipline.

**Worked example — correctly routed:**
Interlocutor cites the Sanaa manuscript as evidence that the Qurʾān has a "lost earlier version." Correct provenance step: establish that the Sanaa fragments show orthographic variants and scribal practices consistent with early Qurʾānic copying, not a doctrinally different text. Correct contents step: the variants are within known authorized reading categories, not evidence of different doctrine. Correct authority step: even if the manuscript were formally earlier than currently held, the epistemic weight of mass mutawātir transmission does not reside in any single manuscript. The artifact cannot overturn the transmission.

**Worked example — incorrectly routed (wrong-tradition assumptions imported):**
Response treats the Sanaa fragments as equivalent to a NT papyrus find, applies Westcott-Hort textual criticism (reconstructive skepticism, preference for earlier single witness over later mass testimony), and concludes that the Qurʾān must be "reconstructed" from physical artifacts rather than received through transmission. Error: this imports the conditions and method of NT text criticism — which was developed for a tradition with documented transmission gaps — onto a tradition whose preservation conditions are categorically different.

## RT-2 — Canon Formation vs. Inspired Authority

V10 sequence governs: maintain provenance → contents → authority ordering (`references/techniques/V10-transmission-content-vetting.md`).

*The specific question this case answers:* Does the historical process of canon recognition undermine the authority of the recognized text?
*Evidence-type this operates on:* Canon-formation history, list-making events, community debates, competing texts.

**The pressure:** "Communities selected texts later, so scripture has no binding authority" or
"canon instability proves revelation is only communal politics," including original-text
skepticism when the point being pressed is authority rather than reconstruction.

**Primary distinction:** The historical process by which a community recognizes a canon is not
identical to the question of whether a text is inspired, revelatory, or normatively authoritative.

*Sub-route 1 — Provenance:* What historical process is being cited? Distinguish: (a) the initial reception and preservation of revelation; (b) the process of community recognition of which texts are authoritative; (c) the administrative compilation of an authorized codex. These are three different events. The interlocutor often conflates them.

*Sub-route 2 — Contents:* What did the canon-recognition process actually debate? Debates about which texts to include do not show that no texts have privileged status — they show that the community took the question of authority seriously. Debate about recognition is not the same as the generation of authority. The debates presuppose that there is something to recognize.

*Sub-route 3 — Authority:* Does community recognition create authority or identify it? The distinction is critical. A tradition's account of how the canon was recognized may differ from the questioner's assumptions about how authority works. The Qurʾānic account is that the community identified and preserved what was received; the authority belongs to what was received, not to the act of recognition.

*Cross-tradition contamination guard:* NT canon formation involved centuries of debate about which texts had apostolic authority, with no single authoritative living community and documented diversity of early canons. The Qurʾānic situation is categorically different: the community had direct access to the Prophet, an authoritative recitational tradition, and a documented compilation process within a generation. Do not import the NT canon-formation conditions and their epistemological implications onto the Qurʾānic case.

**Operational notes:**
- A list-making event does not create revelation out of nothing.
- Debate about canon recognition does not prove equal status for all competing texts.
- Reconstruction, community recognition, inspiration, and authority must be separated.

**Restorative point:** The case must name which level is under dispute: recognition-history, textual reconstruction, revelatory status, or authority. Without that separation the objection looks larger than it is.

**Worked example — correctly routed:**
Interlocutor asks: "Who decided which surahs belonged in the Qurʾān?" Correct provenance step: identify the compilation history — the documented gathering of written and memorized text under Abū Bakr and the authorized codex under ʿUthmān. Correct contents step: the process was recognition of what was authentically received, not creation of content. Correct authority step: the authority of the text derives from the revelation received, not from the administrative act of compilation.

**Worked example — incorrectly routed (NT canon-formation assumptions imported):**
Response treats the ʿUthmānic compilation as analogous to the Council of Nicaea determining the NT canon (a centuries-later institutional decision about which texts to recognize), imports the assumption that compilation = community-created authority, and concludes that the Qurʾān is a community product rather than a received revelation. Error: the NT canon was disputed over centuries with a genuine diversity of early canons; the Qurʾānic compilation process, within a generation of the Prophet, under the supervision of Companions who had direct knowledge of the revelation, is a categorically different event.

## RT-3 — Qurʾānic Preservation, Qirāʾāt, and Manuscript Confusion

V10 sequence governs: maintain provenance → contents → authority ordering (`references/techniques/V10-transmission-content-vetting.md`).

*The specific question this case answers:* Does the plurality of qirāʾāt or manuscript diversity indicate textual corruption?
*Evidence-type this operates on:* Qirāʾāt (authorized recitational readings), aḥruf (the seven modes), manuscript orthography, early codex variation.

**The pressure:** "qirāʾāt, aḥruf, manuscripts, or early codices prove corruption / instability."

**Primary distinction:** Variant readings, modes of recitation, manuscript witnesses, and claims of textual corruption are not interchangeable categories.

*Sub-route 1 — Provenance:* What category of variation is being cited? Qirāʾāt are authorized recitational readings traced to the Prophet through mutawātir chains — they are not scribal accidents. Aḥruf are the seven modes in which the Qurʾān was revealed, each authorized. Early manuscript orthographic variation reflects pre-standardized Arabic script conventions, not doctrinal difference. These are three distinct categories; conflating them is the central confusion.

*Sub-route 2 — Contents:* What does the cited variation actually contain? Early Qurʾānic manuscripts lack vowel markers and dotting by convention — this is not evidence of ambiguity in the text but an artifact of early Arabic script. The meaning carried by the recitational tradition is not created by manuscript markers; it is transmitted through the living oral tradition. Orthographic variation in manuscripts that lack full diacritics does not generate doctrinal variation.

*Sub-route 3 — Authority:* Is the variation within the authorized transmission system or outside it? Qirāʾāt that are mutawātir are authoritative; those that are not are distinguished by the tradition itself. The tradition has developed precise categories for what counts as an authorized reading. The interlocutor who imports NT text-critical reasoning expects to find uncontrolled scribal variation; the Qurʾānic tradition's preservation system produced authorized plurality within a controlled framework.

*Cross-tradition contamination guard:* NT manuscript variants include scribal harmonization, doctrinal adjustments, and copyist errors documented across a fragmentary manuscript tradition without a living oral transmission. Qurʾānic qirāʾāt are not this — they are authorized modes of recitation traced through named chains to the Prophet, chosen by authoritative reciters from authentic options, not scribal accidents. Reading qirāʾāt as equivalent to NT manuscript variants is a category error. The method of NT textual criticism (reconstructing an "original text" from divergent manuscript witnesses) was built for conditions that do not obtain here.

**Worked example — correctly routed:**
Interlocutor cites variation between Ḥafṣ and Warsh readings as evidence of "two different Qurʾāns." Correct provenance step: both are named qirāʾāt traced through mutawātir chains — authorized recitational traditions. Correct contents step: the differences are phonological and recitational, not doctrinal. Correct authority step: both readings are recognized as authentic by the Islamic scholarly tradition; the plurality is authorized, not uncontrolled.

**Worked example — incorrectly routed (NT text-critical heuristics imported):**
Response treats Ḥafṣ vs. Warsh variation as equivalent to a Codex Sinaiticus vs. Codex Vaticanus discrepancy in NT manuscripts — implying that one is more "original" and the other is a later scribal development, and that a "critical text" should be reconstructed from the divergent witnesses. Error: this imports NT text-critical methodology (reconstruction of an "original" from divergent manuscripts) onto a tradition that does not have divergent manuscripts in that sense — it has authorized recitational plurality within a disciplined transmission system.

## RT-4 — Muslim-Internal Textual-Historical Destabilization

*The specific question this case answers:* How should a believer who has been destabilized by text-history pressure be engaged?
*Evidence-type this operates on:* The believer's own internal state; the source-material that has destabilized them; their prior belief-structure.

**The pressure:** A believer is shaken by text-history material, canon discourse, manuscript clips,
or Qurʾānic-preservation claims and begins to confuse destabilization with refutation.

**Primary distinction:** This is often not a neutral historical question. It may be a mixed case of
text-history confusion, authority fatigue, and panic over inherited-but-unexamined belief.

*Sub-route 1 — Provenance:* Classify the source-pressure with V10. What type of material has destabilized the believer — manuscript, qirāʾāt, canon-history, or a secondary source presenting these? The source-type determines which RT case the content belongs to and which counter-route is needed.

*Sub-route 2 — Contents:* Identify whether the panic is about transmission reliability, authority grounds, or loss of confidence in teachers and community. These are different problems: transmission reliability routes through RT-1 and RT-3; authority grounds route through RT-2; loss of confidence in teachers is an authority-fatigue case (NS-8 profile), not a text-history case.

*Sub-route 3 — Authority:* Route to P5 when examined conviction needs strengthening, not only case rebuttal. The goal is not to score against an external critic but to rebuild the believer's examined confidence in the right article of faith.

*Cross-tradition contamination guard:* The believer who has been exposed to NT-derived historical criticism applied to the Qurʾān has been given a framework calibrated to a different transmission tradition. Part of the pastoral and intellectual work is naming that the method being applied does not match the subject matter. This must be done without dismissiveness — the believer may have encountered real scholars whose arguments deserve engagement — but with precision about why the method is being applied outside its conditions.

**Operational sequence:**
1. Classify the source-pressure with V10.
2. Identify whether the panic is about transmission, authority, or loss of confidence in teachers.
3. Route to P5 when examined conviction needs strengthening, not only case rebuttal.

**Restorative point:** The goal is not to score against an external critic. It is to separate what has actually been shown from what fear has filled in, then rebuild examined confidence in the right article of faith.

**Worked example — correctly routed:**
Believer has watched a YouTube video claiming qirāʾāt prove the Qurʾān was corrupted. Correct provenance step: identify what type of claim the video makes (RT-3: qirāʾāt confusion). Correct contents step: explain what qirāʾāt actually are (authorized recitational traditions, not scribal variants). Correct authority step: the believer's examined conviction needs strengthening — route to P5 alongside RT-3 content. Pastoral register governs: address the distress first, doctrinal content second.

**Worked example — incorrectly routed:**
Response treats the RT-4 case as a standard external debate prompt — deploys technical text-critical counter-arguments without acknowledging the believer's destabilized state, without establishing whether the concern is about transmission, authority, or teacher-confidence, and without pastoral sequencing. Error: answering an internal crisis as if it were only a debate prompt. The governing register is RT-4 (internal, pastoral) before it is RT-1 or RT-3 (technical, external).

## Surface-Marker Triage — RT-1 vs. RT-2 vs. RT-3 vs. RT-4

RT cases are commonly misrouted because the surface material (a fragment, a variant, a
canon-history claim) looks similar across the four. The correct routing is determined
not by the material but by what the material is being asked to do in the objection.

| If the interlocutor is asking... | Route to | Telltale |
|----------------------------------|----------|----------|
| "This artifact proves the text was reconstructed later / the original is lost" | RT-1 | The artifact is positioned as self-authenticating; the interlocutor does not distinguish reconstruction from transmission |
| "Canon was chosen by community politics, so the text has no binding authority" | RT-2 | The live claim is about authority/inspiration, not reconstruction; the artifact-question is downstream |
| "qirāʾāt / aḥruf / codex differences show textual corruption" | RT-3 | Recitational-plurality material is being read as scribal-variance material; category error between authorized reading and manuscript accident |
| "I'm a Muslim and this is destabilizing me" | RT-4 | The register is internal crisis, not external debate; the pastoral order applies before the doctrinal |

**Ambiguous surface cases:**
- A manuscript claim that is *really* an authority claim → RT-2, not RT-1. Test: ask the
  interlocutor what would change if the manuscript were authenticated as they describe;
  if the answer is "then the authority claim is refuted," the case is RT-2.
- A qirāʾāt claim that is *really* a reconstruction claim → RT-1 plus V10 on the method,
  because the interlocutor is importing a reconstruction framework onto recitational
  material.
- A canon-history claim raised *to destabilize a believer* → RT-4 first, then RT-2 after
  the pastoral ground is held.

The rule: RT-1 is about the artifact's evidential status; RT-2 is about the authority's
grounding; RT-3 is about the distinction between recitation-category and manuscript-
category; RT-4 is about register (internal crisis). Do not collapse them.

## Minimal-Pair Discriminator: RT-1 vs. RT-2

*Decision rule:* RT-1 is about the provenance and reliability of the transmitted text — whether the chain of transmission and the artifact are trustworthy. RT-2 is about the authority and legitimacy of the canon — which texts have binding authority and on what grounds. These are orthogonal: a text can be reliably transmitted (RT-1 clean) and still contested as to its authority (RT-2 live).

*Left correct (RT-1):* Interlocutor challenges whether the Qurʾān as we have it reflects the original recitation — citing manuscript fragments, alleged variants, or transmission chains. The pressure is on the reliability of the transmission process itself, not on whether the text has authority. RT-1 is live; route to provenance/transmission vetting. Not RT-2 — the interlocutor is not questioning which texts have authority but whether the text as received is trustworthy.

*Right correct (RT-2):* Interlocutor grants that the Qurʾān is reliably transmitted but asks: "Who decided which surahs belonged and why should I accept that decision?" The pressure is on canonization — the process by which the text was assembled and its authority legitimated. RT-2 is live; route to canon-formation and authority-source vetting. Not RT-1 — the interlocutor is not questioning transmission reliability but authority and inclusion.

*Differentiating signal:* Is the pressure on "did this text reach us accurately?" (RT-1) or "why does this text have authority?" (RT-2)? The two can co-occur; when they do, provenance must precede authority — a text cannot bear authority claims before its reliability is addressed.
