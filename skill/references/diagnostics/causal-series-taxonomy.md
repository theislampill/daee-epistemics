# Causal-Series Taxonomy

This file prevents a common collapse: every appeal to regress, infinity, or causation gets treated as the same problem. It is not.

The governing question is always: **what kind of series is live, what sort of impossibility is being asserted, and what role are secondary causes being made to play?**

---

## Classification Grid

### 1. Simultaneous vs. successive series

**Successive series:** earlier members are gone when later members operate.
- Example form: historical sequence, temporal succession, one event after another.
- Typical pressure: "an infinite past is impossible" or "an endless chain of earlier causes explains nothing."

**Simultaneous dependency series:** later members depend here-and-now on what is concurrently sustaining them.
- Example form: instrumental or derivative causes whose efficacy is not self-standing.
- Typical pressure: "secondary causes cannot be self-sufficient" or "a merely derivative chain does not generate independent causal power."

**Route consequence:** If the issue is simultaneous dependency, do not answer as though the case were only about the length of a past sequence. The question is present dependence, not merely temporal count.

### 2. Causal regress vs. numerical infinity

**Causal regress:** explanatory or ontological dependence is being deferred without a self-sufficient source.

**Numerical infinity:** the discussion is only about whether a sequence can be infinitely many in count.

These are not identical. A case may deny one, the other, both, or neither.

**Route consequence:** If the interlocutor is only pressing numerical infinity, do not pretend a causal-dependence objection has already been granted. If the interlocutor is pressing causal dependence, do not let the case collapse into arithmetic alone.

### 3. Circularity types

**Direct causal circularity:** A depends on B while B depends on A in the same respect.

**Explanatory circularity:** the conclusion is used to supply the explanatory ground that was needed to justify it.

**Definitional circularity:** the key term is defined in a way that quietly assumes the result to be proven.

**Route consequence:** Definitional circularity routes to definition-discipline.md. Causal or explanatory circularity stays here.

### 4. Primary vs. secondary causes

**Primary cause:** not derivative in its efficacy from another concurrent source.

**Secondary cause:** real, operative, and efficacious — but not self-sufficient. Its efficacy is derivative, sustained, or conditioned.

**Critical rule:** The reality of secondary causes does not grant them self-sufficiency. "Natural causes exist" and "nature is causally self-explanatory" are different claims.

**Route consequence:** When the live move is to turn secondary causes into an ultimate explanatory closure, this file governs before generic naturalist rebuttal or generic usurpation language.

## When This File Governs Instead of Generic Usurpation

Use this file as the primary diagnostic owner when:

- the live pressure is a series-classification mistake
- the dispute turns on derivative vs. self-sufficient causality
- a regress argument is being made at the level of causal structure rather than tribunal-installation

Use philosophical-usurpation.md first when:

- the causal grammar itself is being installed as the tribunal revelation must satisfy
- necessity/contingency or Aristotelian causality is functioning as an upstream authority rather than as a specific argument

In mixed cases, keep both live: tribunal-clearing first, series-classification second.

## Minimal-Pair Discriminators

**Minimal pair 1 — successive vs. simultaneous**
- "There cannot be infinitely many earlier temporal events." → successive-series pressure
- "A purely derivative chain has no self-standing causal source right now." → simultaneous-dependency pressure

**Minimal pair 2 — causal regress vs. numerical infinity**
- "An infinite count is impossible." → numerical-infinity claim
- "Endless deferral never yields explanatory sufficiency." → causal-regress claim

**Minimal pair 3 — secondary causes vs. self-sufficiency**
- "There are natural causes." → ordinary secondary-cause affirmation
- "Therefore nature is explanatorily complete in itself." → self-sufficiency overreach

## Failure Tests

This file has not governed the response if:

- a simultaneous-dependency case is answered only with past-infinity arithmetic
- numerical infinity is treated as though it automatically proved causal self-sufficiency false
- secondary causes are refuted instead of distinguished from self-sufficient primary causality
- a causal-series case is routed only through philosophical-usurpation.md without adding any series classification
