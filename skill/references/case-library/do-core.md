### DO-1 — Divine Hiddenness

**The objection:** God, if he exists, desires relationship with all human beings. A loving God
would ensure that all sincere seekers come to know him. But many sincere seekers remain
unconvinced despite genuine inquiry. Therefore God either does not exist or does not desire
universal relationship.

**Strongest rebuttal to the Islamic response:**
The typical response invokes the fiṭrah — hiddenness is an illusion because God has already made
himself known through the fiṭrah. The rebuttal: this assumes rather than demonstrates that
sincere seekers have a functioning fiṭrah available. For someone who has genuinely sought and
found nothing, attributing the failure to a defect in the seeker rather than absence of the sought
object is an unfalsifiable immunizing move. It appears to make the theory non-disconfirmable.

**Taymiyyan response to the rebuttal:**
The rebuttal misidentifies the structure of the argument. The claim is not a psychological
generalization that could be falsified by counter-examples — it is a claim about the conditions
of proper function. The fiṭrah's correct operation requires not merely cognitive effort but a
specific moral-spiritual condition of the qalb.

This is not unfalsifiable because the conditions are independently specifiable. The tradition
identifies with precision what suppresses the fiṭrah: the seven deformations. These are not
post-hoc rationalizations — they are pre-specified conditions examinable in the particular case.
The question to the interlocutor is not "were you sincere enough?" but "what was the actual
moral and existential condition of your search?" — the presence of gharaḍ, of hawā, of shubha,
of ʿāda are independently identifiable and their presence in the search can be examined.

Furthermore: the premise "if God exists and desires relationship, all sincere seekers will be
convinced" assumes a particular model of divine relationship the tradition does not endorse. The
tradition's model is not that God overwhelms the faculties of the seeker with irresistible
evidence. God has placed the capacity for recognition within the human constitution itself. The
hiddenness is not God's concealment but the seeker's occlusion — and the tradition is precise
about what occludes.

**Remaining probe:** The interlocutor may claim to have met all specified conditions and still
found nothing. The appropriate response here is not further argument but the maieutic procedure
(P4) — attend carefully to what actually happened in the search versus what is now being
remembered through the lens of a deconversion narrative.

### DO-2 — The Problem of Evil (Evidential)

**The objection:** The amount, intensity, and distribution of suffering — especially natural evil
not caused by human choice — constitutes strong evidence against an all-powerful, all-knowing,
perfectly good God. Even if not strictly logically incompatible, it significantly lowers the
probability of God's existence.

**Strongest rebuttal to the Islamic response:**
Standard theistic responses appeal to soul-making, free will, or unknown divine reasons (skeptical
theism). The rebuttal to skeptical theism: if we cannot reason from the apparent pointlessness of
suffering to its actual pointlessness because God might have reasons we cannot fathom, we cannot
reason inductively at all — this undercuts the evidential base for science, history, and ordinary
life. Skeptical theism purchases theodicy at the cost of reason itself.

**Taymiyyan response to the rebuttal:**
The rebuttal conflates two distinct epistemic claims. The claim "we cannot always know God's
reasons for permitting suffering" is a claim about the limits of human knowledge of divine
purposes, grounded in the ontological distance between Creator and creature. The claim "we cannot
reason inductively from observational data" is a claim about the general reliability of inductive
reasoning. These are not equivalent, and collapsing them is a logical error.

The tradition's position is not that human reason cannot reach reliable conclusions from
observable data — it can, and does. The position is that one specific domain — the full range of
divine purposes in permitting particular events — exceeds what human reason operating on
observable data can reach. This is targeted epistemic modesty, not a blanket abandonment of
inductive reasoning.

Moreover: M2 (prior probability) — the evidential problem of evil is probabilistic. Probability
assessments require a prior. The Taymiyyan prior probability of God's existence is not near-zero;
it is grounded in the fiṭrah's basic deliverances, the convergence of three knowledge sources,
and the tawātur fiṭrī of theistic recognition. When this prior is properly accounted for, the
evidential weight of suffering does not do the claimed work.

Finally, and most directly: the tradition identifies suffering as an āyah — a sign, not an
embarrassment. Death, loss, and the fragility of worldly goods are among the occasions through
which the fiṭrah's orientation toward the permanent is most activated.

**Grief register note (M4):** When the problem of evil is presented from within personal loss,
the argumentative register must be entirely suspended. Address the grief first, directly,
genuinely. Theological content, if it comes at all, comes later. The fiṭrah of a person in genuine
grief is often very close to the surface — argument cannot reach it; genuine presence can.

### DO-3 — The Evolutionary Debunking of Religious Belief

**The objection:** Religious belief can be fully explained by evolutionary processes that enhanced
survival and reproductive fitness, without reference to truth. If a belief is fully explained by
a process not aimed at truth, the belief is epistemically unreliable. Therefore religious belief
is epistemically unreliable.

**Strongest rebuttal to the Islamic response:**
The Islamic response distinguishes the evolutionary origin of a capacity (the fiṭrah) from its
reliability. The rebuttal: scientific beliefs have external verification through empirical testing
that religious beliefs lack. The evolutionary debunking argument is therefore more damaging to
religious belief than to scientific belief, even if both are evolutionary products.

**Taymiyyan response to the rebuttal (M1 — self-refutation):**
The supposed asymmetry does not hold. The claim that "external verification" means "empirically
testable against physical-world data" smuggles in the naturalist's evidential criterion as if
established, when it is precisely the premise under dispute.

More fundamentally: the evolutionary debunking argument, if successful, succeeds universally.
The faculties used to evaluate the argument — the capacity for logical reasoning, trust in
scientific testimony, the intuition that truth-tracking is a virtue — are themselves evolutionary
products aimed at fitness rather than truth. The argument that evolution-shaped beliefs are
unreliable is itself an evolution-shaped belief. This is not a peripheral problem — it is a
self-refutation built into the argument's structure. State it cleanly and early: the argument
cannot be asserted without using the very cognitive faculties it declares unreliable.

The positive Taymiyyan account: the fiṭrah was created by God to orient human beings toward
recognition of God, and its cross-cultural, involuntary, persistent outputs are exactly what one
would expect if this account is correct. The evolutionary explanation and the theological
explanation are not mutually exclusive — God can work through secondary causes, including the
biological structures of human cognition, to produce the dispositions he intends.

### DO-4 — The Problem of Religious Diversity

**The objection:** Many mutually incompatible religious traditions exist, each producing sincere
believers. If the fiṭrah were a reliable guide to religious truth, we would expect convergence
rather than divergence. Diversity is evidence that religious belief is culturally produced, not
fiṭrah-delivered.

**Strongest rebuttal to the Islamic response:**
The Islamic response distinguishes the fiṭrah-deliverable (generic theism: one God, moral
obligation, accountability) from the doctrinal superstructure that varies across traditions.
The rebuttal: if the fiṭrah only delivers generic theism, it cannot justify the specific claims
of Islam — the prophethood of Muḥammad, the Qurʾān as divine revelation. The jump from generic
theism to specifically Islamic commitments requires independent justification the fiṭrah cannot
provide.

**Taymiyyan response to the rebuttal:**
The rebuttal correctly identifies the epistemological structure — and the tradition's actual
account matches it. The fiṭrah delivers generic theistic recognition: the existence of a Creator,
the reality of moral obligation, human accountability. The specific claims of the Islamic
tradition — prophethood, revelation, specific theological commitments — are grounded in the second
knowledge source: reliable testimony (khabar) reaching the level of tawātur, combined with
rational reflection (naẓar ʿaqlī) on the internal coherence and distinctive character of the
prophetic message.

This is not a concession — it is the tradition's actual epistemological structure. Fiṭrah is the
anchor; khabar and naẓar build upon it. Religious diversity is not evidence against the fiṭrah's
deliverances — it is evidence that the seven deformations operate differently in different
contexts, producing divergent elaborations on a common foundation. The convergent core — a
Creator, moral accountability, the inadequacy of the purely material — is remarkably stable
across traditions. Divergence is in the superstructure; convergence in the foundation.

Furthermore: the expectation of uniform convergence is unrealistic given the tradition's own
account. The fiṭrah is not incorruptible — it is obscured by the seven deformations, which
operate differently in different cultural and historical contexts. The diversity of religious belief
is precisely what the tradition predicts, given its account of how the fiṭrah operates in a world
where occlusion is widespread.

### DO-5 — The Argument from Divine Transcendence Against Language

Technique: V8-bila-kayf-anchor.md governs attribute-discipline and transcendence framing for this objection.

**The objection:** If God is truly transcendent — infinite, beyond human categories — then any
revelation mediated through human language is necessarily distorted. Human language is formed
from finite, creaturely experience and cannot accurately represent infinite reality. Therefore
either revelation is not genuinely from God, or it is radically different from how it is
understood. Either way, specific doctrinal commitments rest on an incoherent foundation.

**Strongest rebuttal to the Islamic response:**
The Islamic response distinguishes knowing God's essence (unknowable) from knowing his attributes
as he has described them (possible through revelation). The rebuttal: this distinction does not
escape the problem. If human concepts are formed from creaturely experience, then "mercy,"
"knowledge," "power," "will" as applied to God are derived from creaturely instances and cannot,
without distortion, apply to an infinite being. If the concepts are systematically disanalogous,
propositional content becomes either false or empty.

**Taymiyyan response to the rebuttal (M1 — self-refutation + V8 — bilā kayf):**
The rebuttal assumes that language is meaningful only if it refers by identical correspondence —
that "knowledge" applied to God must mean exactly what it means applied to a human being, and
any deviation from this makes the application empty. This assumption is false.

Terms carry a semantic core — "knowledge" denotes the property of a subject such that things are
as they are to that subject — that applies across different kinds of instances while the mode of
instantiation varies. God knows; his knowledge is real knowledge, not metaphor. But the *how*
(kayfiyyah) of his knowing is unlike the *how* of creaturely knowing. This is how language about
any sufficiently different kind of being works.

M1: The objection is self-defeating. If human language cannot refer to anything beyond creaturely
experience, then "God is incomprehensible" cannot be stated without using creaturely language to
make a claim about what exceeds creaturely language. The objection cannot be formulated without
the very applicability it denies.

Bilā kayf positions itself between two errors: tashbīh (applying creaturely mode to divine
attributes) and tāʿṭīl (denying the reality of divine attributes). Both are worse than the
epistemically honest acknowledgment of the limit of creaturely cognition applied to the infinite.

**DO-5 / DO-13 routing note:** For the related DO-13 case (philosopher's-God vs. revelatory-God), where predication is accepted but a philosophical ontological model is pressed against revelation, see `references/case-library/do-christian-extensions.md`. DO-5 and DO-13 require a discriminator: DO-5 is a semantics-of-language problem (whether predication is coherent at all); DO-13 is a competing-ontology problem (which model of God is correct). See the minimal-pair discriminator block at the end of the do-christian-extensions file.

### DO-6 — The Coherence of the Classical Concept of God

Technique: V8-bila-kayf-anchor.md governs attribute-discipline and transcendence framing for this objection.

**The objection:** The classical attributes of God — omniscience, omnipotence, omnibenevolence,
necessary existence — are either individually incoherent or mutually contradictory. E.g.:
omniscience requires knowing future free actions, but if those actions are genuinely free they
are not yet determined and therefore not knowable. Omnipotence implies the ability to create a
stone too heavy for an omnipotent being to lift — contradiction regardless of the answer. These
show the concept of God, as classically formulated, is incoherent.

**Strongest rebuttal to the Islamic response:**
The Islamic response rejects the specific formulation of divine attributes in Western philosophical
theology as a distortion not representative of the Qurʾānic account. The rebuttal: this may be
internally consistent but it is evasive. If the Islamic tradition has a coherent account of divine
knowledge, power, and goodness, it must engage the logical puzzles on their own terms.

**Taymiyyan response to the rebuttal:**
The rebuttal is correct that the tradition must engage the puzzles. The engagement reveals that
each puzzle rests on an equivocation.

*The omnipotence puzzle:* Assumes omnipotence means "the ability to perform any logically
specifiable action, including self-contradictory ones." The tradition's account: omnipotence is
the perfection of power — the ability to do anything the doing of which is coherent.
Self-contradictory descriptions (a stone too heavy for an omnipotent being to lift) are not
descriptions of possible states of affairs — they are grammatically well-formed sentences
describing nothing. Not a constraint on divine power; a grammatical confusion.

*The omniscience-freedom puzzle:* Divine knowledge is not temporally structured knowledge of a
succession of events as future, present, and past. God is not located in time in the way his
creatures are. The temporal framework within which the tension between foreknowledge and freedom
arises is a creaturely framework. Applying it to divine knowledge is the same mode-error addressed
in DO-5 and V8 (`references/techniques/V8-bila-kayf-anchor.md`): confusing the how of creaturely
cognition with the how of divine cognition. God's
knowledge of a free action does not coerce the agent, any more than present knowledge of a past
free action retroactively determines what was freely done.

*The existence-as-predicate puzzle* (Kant's objection to the ontological argument): Directed at
the Anselmian ontological argument — not at the cosmological argument, not at the tradition's
primary epistemological grounds (fiṭrah, āyāt, tawātur). The tradition's primary framework does
not depend on the ontological argument. Treat this objection as targeted at an argument the
tradition does not primarily rely upon; redirect to the actual grounds.
