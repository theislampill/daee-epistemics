# Attribute Discourse Precision — Named Confusions and Fixtures

The Arabic corpus turns repeatedly on subtle distinctions in what is being affirmed, denied, predicated, or analogized about God. Most failures in attribute discourse occur not at the level of doctrine but at the level of the logical move being made — a category is crossed, an analogy is extended past its valid range, a predication-type is conflated with another, or a composition argument is built on an equivocation. These fixtures isolate the move, name the failure mode, and specify what the corrected operation looks like.

Each entry: named confusion, mechanism, fixture (bad output / corrected output), self-audit question, prohibited move.

---

## Confusion 1 — Category Mistake in Predication

**Mechanism:** Attributes that apply to finite, composite, or caused entities are predicated of God using the same logical operation as if the predication-type were the same. The mistake is not in affirming the attribute but in importing the finite-entity predication structure alongside it.

**Common form:** "If God has knowledge, then knowledge is an attribute distinct from God's essence, hence God is composite, hence God is dependent — which contradicts divine perfection." This argument treats "having knowledge" as the same kind of predication for God as for a created knowing subject: a predicated accident added to a substrate.

**Fixture — bad output:**
"The objection is correct that divine knowledge is distinct from the divine essence; we resolve this by saying the attribute is identical with the essence in some sense." — This accepts the predication structure of the objection and tries to patch it.

**Fixture — corrected output:**
"The predication-type is wrong before the conclusion is drawn. 'Having knowledge' in creaturely discourse names the acquisition of an accident by a substrate. When the tradition affirms divine knowledge, it does not affirm that God acquired an accident — it affirms that the divine essence is such that it knows, in a manner that befits His majesty and transcends the finite category. The predication structure appropriate to creatures does not apply: God's knowledge is not an accident of God. The objection's conclusion (God is composite because knowledge is distinct from essence) is a category mistake — it imports the finite predication structure into the transcendent domain without justification. The tradition's response is not to define away the distinction but to locate the error in the predication move." See V8 (bilā kayf) for how affirmation is held without assimilation to creaturely categories.

**Self-audit question:** Am I accepting the finite predication structure while trying to adjust the conclusion, or am I identifying the predication move itself as the site of the error?

**Prohibited move:** Accepting "God has attributes distinct from His essence, therefore composite" without identifying the category shift in "has" — this grants the Muʿtazilī or naturalist framing before it has been examined.

## Confusion 2 — Illicit Analogy (Extension Past Valid Range)

**Mechanism:** An analogy between a divine attribute and a creaturely analog is used to generate a conclusion, but the conclusion requires the analogy to hold in a domain where the relevant disanalogies break it.

**Common form:** "God is a person. Persons have bodies, or are spatially located, or are subject to change — therefore God should be detectable, or God's hiddenness is like a person hiding, or God's decisions imply real temporal sequence."

**Fixture — bad output:**
"God is a person in a metaphorical sense, so we shouldn't expect bodily presence." — This concedes the person-framework while adding a qualifier, leaving the analogy in control.

**Fixture — corrected output:**
"The objection extends the person-analogy from what it warrants into what it does not. The tradition affirms that God is personal — knowing, willing, speaking — because these predicates apply truly to God in a sense that befits His transcendence. The analogy to finite persons is legitimate for establishing the category (not impersonal) but does not carry over to the creaturely features that depend on finitude, embodiment, or spatial location. The analogy's valid range is personal-versus-impersonal; it breaks before bodily presence, spatial location, and temporal sequence, which are features of finite personal existence, not of personhood as such. Extending the analogy past its valid range generates the apparent contradiction. Name the extension point; show which disanalogies are operative there."

**Self-audit question:** At exactly what point does the interlocutor's analogy extend past the valid range? Name that point before responding.

**Prohibited move:** Retreating to pure transcendence ("God is not a person at all, only metaphorically") — this concedes the impersonal framework that the tradition rejects. The tradition affirms both transcendence and genuine personal attributes; the task is to hold both without importing the creaturely structure that comes with finite personal existence.

## Confusion 3 — Equivocal Predication Without Marking the Equivocation

**Mechanism:** A predicate term is used in its ordinary creaturely sense when making the objection, then shifted to a qualified transcendent sense when the defense is offered — without the shift being made explicit. Or the reverse: the defense uses a term in the transcendent sense, but the objection's force depended on the ordinary sense, leaving the objection unaddressed.

**Common form:** "God is good. But the things God commands or permits [name case] are not good by any standard I recognize. So either God is not good, or 'good' means something so different for God that the statement is vacuous."

**Fixture — bad output:**
"God's goodness is a different kind of goodness — higher goodness that we don't always understand." — This asserts an equivocation without resolving it or identifying what genuine continuity exists between the two senses.

**Fixture — corrected output:**
"There are two questions being conflated. First: is 'good' predicated of God univocally (the same sense as for creatures), equivocally (entirely different), or analogically (genuinely shared, but with creaturely features stripped)? The tradition holds the analogical position: 'good' applies to God genuinely — not as an empty honorific — but without the limitations, deficiency-based contrasts, or acquisitive structure that 'good' carries in creaturely discourse. Second: the specific case [name it] is a normative dispute about what goodness requires in that context, not evidence that 'good' is vacuous when predicated of God. These questions must be separated. The equivocation objection ('it's vacuous') applies only if no genuine continuity exists between divine and creaturely goodness — which the tradition denies. The normative objection ('this specific act isn't good') must be addressed on its own terms, not smuggled in as if it established the equivocation."

**Self-audit question:** Have I distinguished the predication-mode question (univocal / analogical / equivocal) from the first-order normative question about a specific case?

**Prohibited move:** Claiming pure equivocation ("God's goodness has nothing in common with human goodness") — this collapses the analogical predication tradition into pure agnosticism about divine attributes, which the tradition rejects.

## Confusion 4 — Composition Panic (Many Attributes → Complexity → Dependence)

**Mechanism:** The interlocutor (or a tradition of kalāmic evidentialism) argues that affirming real distinct divine attributes introduces composition (tarkīb) into the divine essence, and that composition implies dependence (iftiqār) on the parts or on whatever holds the parts together — which is incompatible with divine independence (ghinā).

**Common form:** "If God has knowledge, power, will, and life as distinct attributes, then God is composite — and a composite being depends on its components, which contradicts divine aseity."

**The Muʿtazilī/philosophical history:** This argument drove the Muʿtazilah to deny real attribute-existence (the attributes are identical with the essence or are just ways of describing the essence) and drove Aristotelian-influenced theology toward the simplicity doctrine. The tradition's mainstream response is not to deny real attributes but to deny that the attribute-essence relation in God involves the dependence that the composition argument assumes.

**Fixture — bad output:**
"God's attributes are numerically identical with His essence." — This satisfies the anti-composition requirement but collapses the attributes into the essence in a way that makes attribute-language equivocal.

**Fixture — corrected output:**
"The composition argument assumes that the attribute-essence relation in God is the same kind of relation as between a finite substance and its accidents — where the accidents are distinct items that the substance has, and removing them would leave the substrate. But the tradition is not affirming that divine attributes are accidents that God has in that sense. The denial is of accidental composition (ʿaraḍ attaching to a substrate), not of all real attribute-distinction. The argument also assumes that 'complexity' entails 'dependence on components' in a way that God cannot avoid — but this moves from logical distinction to ontological dependence by assuming a specific and contestable account of how attributes relate to essence. Identifying which kind of composition is being asserted, and showing that the dependence inference requires a creaturely model of the part-whole relation, dissolves the argument without requiring attribute-denial." See `references/sound-reason-epistemology.md §6.3` for the full tarkīb-iftiqār treatment.

**Self-audit question:** Which kind of composition (accidental, essential, mereological, conceptual) is actually being asserted? The inference to dependence applies only to some of these, not all.

**Prohibited move:** Accepting "many attributes = composite = dependent" without distinguishing the kinds of composition and the conditions under which each entails dependence. This is the error that drove both Muʿtazilī attribute-denial and over-committed philosophical simplicity doctrine.

## Confusion 5 — Attribute-Multiplicity / Person-Multiplicity Conflation (Trinity-Specific)

**Mechanism:** In Christian Trinitarian discourse, the logical structure of "one divine essence, three persons, each fully divine" is evaluated using the same logical framework that governs attribute-multiplicity disputes in general Islamic theology. But the problems are structurally distinct: attribute-multiplicity in Islamic theology is a question about the relation between the essence and its qualifications; person-multiplicity in Trinitarian theology is a question about whether the divine essence can be numerically instantiated in three distinct ways simultaneously. Conflating them generates both incorrect critiques and missed critiques.

**Common form (incorrect conflation):** "The Trinity fails for the same reason that divine attribute-multiplicity is problematic — multiple things in God." — This misidentifies the logical site of the Trinitarian difficulty.

**Fixture — bad output:**
Engaging the Trinity objection as if it were a composition-panic argument (Confusion 4). "The Trinity introduces composition into God, which generates dependence."

**Fixture — corrected output:**
"The Trinitarian difficulty is not primarily a composition argument — it is an identity argument. The question is not whether three properties or attributes can coexist in a single divine essence (that is a question about attribute-multiplicity, and both Islamic and Trinitarian theology agree that multiple attributes are compatible with divine unity at the essence-level). The question is whether three distinct persons, each of whom is fully God, can each be numerically identical with the one divine essence while being numerically distinct from each other — since if A = C and B = C, then A = B. This is the Leibniz's-Law problem that generates the genuine logical pressure. Address this directly; do not substitute a composition argument that does not locate the difficulty accurately." See `case-library/do-christian-extensions.md` for the full DO-11 profile.

**Self-audit question:** Am I addressing the actual logical structure of the Trinitarian claim (identity across persons) or a composition argument that does not reach it?

**Prohibited move:** Treating the Trinity as if the core problem were composition/dependence rather than numerical identity across persons. This misses the actual site of the logical pressure and produces a response the Trinitarian interlocutor will find irrelevant.

## Confusion 6 — Perfect-Being Theology as Neutral Standard

**Mechanism:** The "perfect-being theology" framework (God is the greatest conceivable being; properties are divine iff they make a being greater) is used as a neutral standard by which to evaluate the attributes affirmed of God in revelation — with the implication that any attribute that does not fit the perfect-being framework must be reinterpreted or denied.

**Common form:** "A perfect being cannot have emotions, change, or respond to prayer — these imply limitation. So divine anger, regret, or responsiveness must be metaphors for something else."

**The tradition's position:** The perfect-being framework is not a neutral starting point — it is a specific philosophical position that inherits Aristotelian (and neo-Platonic) assumptions about what constitutes perfection (self-sufficiency, pure actuality, immutability). These assumptions are not obvious and are not derived from revelation. They generate the conclusion that the God of revelation is anthropomorphic by definition — because the God of revelation speaks, commands, responds, and acts in time. This is not a conclusion the tradition accepts, and the framework that generates it is not the neutral standard it presents itself as.

**Fixture — bad output:**
"Divine anger is a metaphor for God's will to punish." — This concedes the perfect-being framework's demand by eliminating the genuine attribute.

**Fixture — corrected output:**
"The argument assumes that the perfect-being framework is the correct standard against which to evaluate divine attributes. But this framework is not derived from revelation; it is inherited from a specific philosophical tradition that takes pure actuality, immutability, and impassibility as the marks of perfection. The tradition affirms these attributes not because they pass a philosophical perfection-test but because they are affirmed in waḥy in ways that the tradition accepts as genuine, not as metaphors for something philosophically more comfortable. The question is not whether divine responsiveness fits the Aristotelian perfection-framework — it does not, and the tradition does not require it to. The question is whether the Aristotelian perfection-framework has any authority over how revelation describes God. It does not. The framework is one philosophical position; the God of revelation does not owe it deference." See `references/case-library/do-christian-extensions.md` DO-13 for the philosopher's-God vs. God of revelation profile.

**Self-audit question:** Am I treating the perfect-being framework as a legitimate constraint and defending revelation within it, or am I naming the framework as one position among others and refusing to grant it the chair?

**Prohibited move:** Allegorizing or reinterpreting revealed attributes to satisfy the perfect-being standard. This grants the standard authority over the text.
